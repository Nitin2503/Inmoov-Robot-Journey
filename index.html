My InMoov Robot Journey — A Dream Built Layer by Layer

I’m Nitin Ajvilkar. In August 2024, with no electronics background and a heart full of curiosity, I began building an open‑source InMoov humanoid robot inspired by the brilliant French sculptor Gaël Langevin. This is my instructive and inspiring story.

They say every great creation begins with a simple thought: “Can I really do this?” I asked myself that in 2024—and pressed print anyway. What followed is a lesson‑filled journey fueled by open‑source, persistence, and penny‑by‑penny progress.

Lesson 1: Inspiration Is the Spark

The InMoov project—created by French sculptor and designer Gaël Langevin—changed how I see creativity. His vision to make a life‑sized, 3D‑printed humanoid robot accessible to anyone lit the first flame for me. I thought, “If he can share it with the world, I can try to build it.”

Lesson 2: Start With What You Have

I didn’t wait for perfect conditions. I bought a 3D printer—funded penny by penny from my salary—and began printing. Part by part, hands and joints emerged. Within months, about 60% of the mechanical build was complete and a quiet silhouette stood on my desk: full of potential.

Tip: You don’t need to know everything to begin. You learn most while building.

Lesson 3: Every Challenge Is a Classroom

Electronics humbled me. Wires, power, servo control—it all felt like a new language. I burnt a few parts, restarted many times, and kept going. I’m learning Arduino, ESP32, and how to drive servo motors safely and smoothly.

Remember: Failure is feedback. Each mistake pushes you one layer closer to success.

Lesson 4: Passion Beats Perfection

With limited funds and no formal engineering background, progress is slower—but it’s steady. Every tiny movement of a servo feels like a festival at home. The project is 40% incomplete electronically—and 100% alive in spirit.

Lesson 5: Inspire Before You Finish

My biggest motivation is my children. I want them to see that curiosity is power—that you can build bold things without waiting for permission, labels, or degrees. The goal isn’t just a robot; it’s a mindset.

Lesson 6: Open‑Source, Open Mind

InMoov thrives because it’s open. Langevin’s generosity built a global community of makers. Sharing knowledge multiplies possibilities—and that’s the heart of my build too.

Lesson 7: Keep Building, Keep Growing

The robot is a work in progress—and so am I. Each print and wire is a line in my story of patience, focus, and learning by doing. If you have a dream that feels impossible, start anyway. Begin small. Keep moving.

“Dreams grow when you feed them with effort. Knowledge comes when you take the first step. Passion makes the impossible possible.”

Beginner Tips (What I’m Learning)

  • Start cheap, print smart: Begin with PLA and moderate infill to save cost and time.
  • Label everything: Bag parts per section (hand, forearm, head) to avoid confusion later.
  • Power matters: Use a stable power supply sized for your servos; avoid brownouts.
  • One joint at a time: Test each servo with a simple Arduino sketch before final assembly.
  • Keep notes: A small logbook of settings, part revisions, and mistakes saves hours.
  • Join the community: Forums & videos can save you from common pitfalls fast.
